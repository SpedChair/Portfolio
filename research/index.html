Stride Frequency & Duty Factor Biomechanics Study

Analyzed stride parameters and running style patterns in collegiate middle- and long-distance races at competition-relevant speeds.

Role: Research Assistant 204 NCAA Runners 2 Championship Meets Regression Modeling SF R² = 0.477 DF R² = 0.607 WCB 2026
⌄

Methodology

Observed stride variables from publicly available NCAA championship race videos, modeled SF and DF using multiple linear regression, and used standardized residuals to characterize running styles.

Data Collection

Sample included 204 NCAA runners from a 2024 D-I championship and a 2021 D-III championship across 800m, 1500m, 5000m, and 10000m events, with each athlete represented once.

Analysis Workflow

Championship Race Video Observation → SF/DF Variable Extraction → Multiple Linear Regression → Standardized Residual Style Classification → Interpretation

Modeling Framework

Step frequency model used speed, leg length, sex, and speed×sex interaction. Duty factor model was primarily explained by speed. Standardized residuals were mapped to style bins using a dual-axis residual framework.

Key Findings

  • Higher-performing athletes were more likely to use larger steps with longer flight times.
  • Lower-performing athletes were more likely to use longer stance times and average step lengths.
  • Running style distribution varied by performance level, not by sex or event grouping.

Validation & Limits

Method checks against elite biomechanical reports showed low SF and DF error. Limits include video measurement constraints, split-based speed approximation, and single-source D-III meet representation.

Skills Demonstrated

Biomechanics observation, quantitative modeling, statistical interpretation, and technical research communication.

Statistical Controls

Model comparison checks, covariate evaluation, and residual-based style classification for consistency across cohorts.
