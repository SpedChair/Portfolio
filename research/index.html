Stride Frequency & Duty Factor Biomechanics Study

Analyzed stride parameters and running style patterns in collegiate middle- and long-distance races at competition-relevant speeds.

Role: Research Assistant 204 NCAA Runners 2 Championship Meets Regression Modeling SF R² = 0.477 DF R² = 0.607 WCB 2026
⌄

Methodology

Observed stride variables from publicly available NCAA championship race videos, modeled SF and DF using multiple linear regression, and used standardized residuals to characterize running styles.

Data Collection

Sample included 204 NCAA runners from a 2024 D-I championship and a 2021 D-III championship across 800m, 1500m, 5000m, and 10000m events, with each athlete represented once.

Analysis Workflow

Championship Race Video Observation → SF/DF Variable Extraction → Multiple Linear Regression → Standardized Residual Style Classification → Interpretation

Modeling Framework

Step frequency model used speed, leg length, sex, and speed×sex interaction. Duty factor model was primarily explained by speed. Standardized residuals were mapped to style bins using a dual-axis residual framework.

Key Findings

  • Higher-performing athletes were more likely to use larger steps with longer flight times.
  • Lower-performing athletes were more likely to use longer stance times and average step lengths.
  • Running style distribution varied by performance level, not by sex or event grouping.

Validation & Limits

Method checks against elite biomechanical reports showed low SF and DF error. Limits include video measurement constraints, split-based speed approximation, and single-source D-III meet representation.

Skills Demonstrated

Biomechanics observation, quantitative modeling, statistical interpretation, and technical research communication.

Statistical Controls

Model comparison checks, covariate evaluation, and residual-based style classification for consistency across cohorts.

Coordination Patterns During Drop Jumps (OpenCap Kinematic Study)

IRB-approved human-subjects biomechanics study investigating how variations in ground contact time influence coordination, kinematics, and performance metrics in trained collegiate athletes.

Role: Student Co-Investigator Lead Data Processing & Analysis Oct 2025 – Present IRB Approved through Oct 2026 OpenCap + Force Plates 175 Analyzed Trials
⌄

Methodology

Designed and refined an IRB-compliant biomechanics workflow, coordinated testing at the Cregger Center, operated OpenCap collection, validated force plate setup, and processed trial data through structured Python analysis with SPM and FDR-controlled comparisons.

Analysis Workflow

OpenCap + Force Plates → Time Alignment → Feature Extraction → Statistical Testing

Key Findings

  • 175 total analyzed trials: 150 at 60 fps and 25 at 240 fps.
  • 12 FDR-significant metrics identified in frame-rate comparison.
  • 9 FDR-significant metrics retained in both 240-only and FPS-adjusted sensitivity models.
  • Results demonstrated robustness of key coordination metrics while confirming measurable frame-rate effects.
  • Poster accepted for World Congress of Biomechanics 2026.

Skills Demonstrated

Motion-capture workflow development, biomechanics data processing, time-series signal analysis, SPM, FDR correction, IRB-compliant protocol execution, waveform aggregation, scientific figure production, and reproducible research version control.

Statistical Controls

Frame-rate sensitivity testing (60 vs 240 fps), 240-only primary analysis, and all-data adjusted sensitivity models with FDR correction across multiple metrics.

Proof Context

IRB approval documentation and WCB 2026 poster acceptance are archived in project records.
